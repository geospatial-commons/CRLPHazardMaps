Methods

Hazard mapping methodologies

This page describes the methodologies used to for each of the hazards found in the CRLP Hazard Mapping Application.

Flood

The flood hazard map based on flood depth indicates how deep the water is expected to be at each location during a modeled flood. The adopted flood hazard classification for the map is a modified version of those developed by the UK Environmental Agency. In particular:

  • Low hazard: yellow color, depth < 0.49 m
  • Medium hazard: orange color, depth between 0.5 m and 0.99 m
  • High hazard: red color, depth > 1 m

Such maps help people understand where flooding might occur and - particularly - how deep it could be at specific places like roads and settled areas, parcels, or facilities. Deeper water generally means greater damage potential, more road closures, and more challenging emergency access. Flood hazard maps can be used for planning of infrastructure (i.e., vegetative riverbank stabilization, retention ponds, desilting, gabion bank protection), broader town development, and evacuation.

Landslide

The rainfall-induced landslide hazard map shows where slopes are naturally more or less prone to landslides during heavy rain. The map combines information on slope steepness, geology, rainfall data and land cover to classify each location from low to very high susceptibility. This is a screening and planning tool for prioritization of slope stabilization interventions (i.e., drainage, stone and gabion walls, brush layering) or evacuation routes planning. Colors indicate relative susceptibility: greens mark lower susceptibility, yellow is moderate, orange is high, and red is very high.

  • Very low susceptibility: green color, 1% probability of generating a landslide
  • Low susceptibility: yellow color, 2% probability of generating a landslide
  • Moderate susceptibility: orange color, 3% probability of generating a landslide
  • Significant susceptibility: red color, >5% probability of generating a landslide

Avalanche

The avalanche hazard map indicates how strong the force from a potential avalanche could be at different locations, expressed in kilopascals (kPa). Higher-pressure zones mean higher destructive potential on buildings, roads, and people. The resulting map uses colors to delineate hazard zones:

  • Very Low hazard: green color, 1-2.5 kPa: avalanche that can break windows
  • Low hazard: yellow color, 2.6 - 17.5 kPa: avalanche that can push doors
  • Medium hazard: orange color, 17.6 - 65 kPa: avalanche that can cause severe damage to wood-frame structures
  • High hazard: red color, 65.1 - 200 kPa: avalanche that can destroy several buildings
  • Very high hazard: dark red color, > 200 kPa: avalanche that can destroy reinforced concrete buildings

The boundaries and names shown and the designations used on this map do not imply official endorsement or acceptance by the World Bank Group.
